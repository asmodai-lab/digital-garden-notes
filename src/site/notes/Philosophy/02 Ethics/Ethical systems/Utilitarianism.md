> [!info] Principle of utility
> "The greatest good for the greatest number."

Utilitarianism has 3 major branches: Act, Rule, and the irrelevant one. They all agree on the principle of utility, but they disagree on how they define 'good', and therefore they disagree on how to achieve it. 

If you are going to use Utilitarianism in any essay, you are probably going to have to mention both, because they are quite different.

Our two philosophers for this are [[Philosophy/02 Ethics/Ethical systems/Utilitarianism#Jeremy Bentham\|Bentham]] and [[Philosophy/02 Ethics/Ethical systems/Utilitarianism#John Stuart Mill\|J.S. Mill]]

## Rule utilitarianism

Mill disagreed with hedonism â€” he thought good is something other than pleasure.
- he reformulated utilitarianism to reflect that **good =/= pleasure**, and that pleasures aren't all of equal value

He introduced the **theory of utility** â€” which replaced pleasure for happiness. 
>He defined happiness as something which is cultural and spiritual, and not just physical
>>He also distinguished between lower and higher pleasures. 

### Higher and lower pleasures

He took a qualitative approach compared to Bentham's more quantitative approach.

He divided pleasures into higher and lower
- higher pleasures were things such as poetry and music
- lower pleasures were things such as eating and drinking

> [!QUOTE]
> *"it is better to be a human satisfied than a pig dissatisfied."*

> His line of thought was similar to **Epicureans**: a group of intellectuals that thought mental pleasure > bodily pleasure

#### Rules

Note that Mill used the principle of utility not for individual situations, but to come up with rules for all of society: which are then followed. Mill is using the principle of utility as a method for discovering the best way of government: essentially just setting the goal as the principle of utility.
