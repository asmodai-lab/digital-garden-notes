## Thinkers

### Max Stirner & Egoism
Stirner's book is called *The Ego and its Own* (1844) and is mainly a critique of 'the state'.

#### His ideas
##### The ego
- Philosophical ideologies and concepts — such as the state, society, and religion, are artificial constructs which act like 'wheels in the head', causing individuals to misunderstand reality.
	- he calls these illusions 'spooks', because they haunt the individual
- Stirner argues that individuals must cut through the deception of the state and society in order to discover the truth: **that the self-interested and rational individual is the centre of their own moral universe.**
- egoism is therefore achieving self-realization: to be free is to truly be oneself, a.k.a. 'ownness'. 
	- this means one has to rise above society to escape domination

##### Union of egoists
- Stirner advocated for the removal of the state (because it is a spook) and it's replacement with a 'union of egoists' — a voluntary organisation of egoists, who voluntarily come together to rationally serve their own interests.
- It's mutually beneficial, and there is no compulsion to conform to another's will.
> [!quote] ''We two, the State and I, are enemies."

###### Capitalism ain't it, chief
Despite being an individualist, Stirner doesn't like capitalism. He argues that capitalism is like any other form of society, i.e. undermines egoism by forcing individuals to conform to it's ideology. 

The individual is as much a slave to capitalism as it is to the state, as Stirner argues that *work should be fulfilling and purposeful to each individual.*

##### Insurrection, not revolution
Stirner advocated insurrection rather than revolution. By that, he means the ability to rise above government and morality by taking control of oneself. Hence, it's a personal journey, but one with 'revolutionary' consequences.

When enough people withdraw themselves, the state will wither and die.

> [!example] Why does Stirner not want a revolution?
> Stirner argued that a typical revolution requires individuals to overthrow the existing order. But, without any changes to themselves, the new order will be plagued with similar problems to the previous one.
> 
> However, he still argues for **propaganda by the deed**, as violence will help shake the spook of the state. (*Bakunin and Goldsmith also support this.*)

### Pierre-Joseph Proudhon & Mutualism
Proudhon's best known work, '*What is Property?*' (1840) critiqued traditional property rights and collective ownership, instead arguing for mutualism: a cooperative, productive system geared towards need rather than profit, and organised within self-governing communities.

#### Property
- Proudhon distinguished between property (of which the elite use to exploit others) and 'possessions' of ordinary peasants and workers (i.e. your toothbrush, or underwear)
	- Private property is illegitimate because it gives one person power over another (i.e. allows the theft of the profit of other's labour)
	- this is why the state is corrupt: because it exists to protect property rights
> [!info] Here is what he said about property:
> 'Property is theft'
> > referring to the above condition of the theft of other's labour
> 
> the employee of a capitalist is 'subordinated, exploited: his permanent condition is one of obedience.'
> 

But he also said ***"Property is freedom."*** 
-> this refers to the product of one's labour — including their home, tools, and the income received by selling their goods. For Proudhon, the only legitimate source of property is one's own labour.

#### Human nature
Proudhon believed that, as the human race developed, it became capable of rational thought, and gained an innate sense of justice.

He also viewed women as inferior, subservient, and second to men.

#### The rejection of the state
- Proudhon is unique among left-wing anarchists, because he argues for a peaceful transition to a new society, not unlike Stirner's idea of the state eventually collapsing.
	- he rejects a violent revolution for similar reasons to Stirner, but also because violent revolutions tend to beget authoritarian resistance.
- Proudhon's mutualist society would exist in the shell of the state, with interconnected institutions such as a people's bank and a federation.
	- he expected this to be a temporary arrangement, as the state would eventually die once mutualism fully takes root.

Proudhon's concept of revolution was partly similar to Marx's, in that it would be a moral revolution. "***Anarchy is order without power.***"

##### Mutual-aid groups
Proudhon advocated for small-scale property ownership, in which workers controlled their own industries, and collect into 'mutual-aid groups', which are essentially social safety networks. 

They are co-operatives, not collectives: because once a group becomes too big, individuals won't have enough say. It's very much still got roots in individualism.

##### The economy
Trade without private property is still possible, but it depends on a few key things:
1. goods should be sold at cost-price
2. 'labour notes': value is based on time/labour put into something
3. a people's mutual-credit bank, which organises this.

#### Critiques of mutualism

| Criticism                                      | Explanation                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              |
| ---------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| A flawed perception of the role of the state   | Proudhon argued that liberty would exist in the shell of the state. However, despite safeguards (such as a federal assembly) to ensure larger communities couldn't dominate smaller ones, it's difficult to disagree that such an arrangement would not see the re-emergence of a state.                 |
| Not reconciling individualism and collectivism | Proudhon struggles to reconcile individualist and collectivist ideas. Ultimately, the collectivist vision wins, as the federal assembly would take precedence over individual's decisions, undermining individual autonomy                                                                               |
| Mutual aid: Unscientific                       | Kropotkin's 'mutual aid' has been accused of being scientifically inaccurate & cherry picking. Likewise, his pre-capitalistic history of the world is idealised to fit his hypothesis. His anthropological examples are also highly selective, only considering evidence that fits his theories.         |
| Mutual aid: Unconsidered assumptions           | Kropotkin assumes mutual aid will re-emerge once the state collapses. He doesn't consider that humans could well have been conditioned to be state-dependent. He also fails to consider that individuals conditioned by the state to be selfish may not act altruistically once the state is vanquished. |

### Mikhail Bakunin & collectivism
Bakunin was born an aristocrat, and was a Russian political agitator and revolutionary. He was initially influenced by Marx, but broke from socialism because he disagreed with the worker's state, because he believed it to be corrupting. His key work was '*God and the State*' (1882).

#### Authority
Bakunin argued that political power was intrinsically oppressive. His anarchism stems from the notion that all authority must be rejected, as submission to the state enslaves people: **liberty** can only be achieved by rejecting the state.

##### Human nature
He argued that humans are naturally social creatures, shaped by society, and can only achieve liberty through the society they live in — never outside society.

He also argued that collective work was a way to be liberated, as **social solidarity** is the key to human nature.

##### Freedom
He thought that freedom could only be achieved through 'collectivism': self-governing communities based on voluntary cooperation, & and the abolition of private property and its replacement with collective ownership, **as liberty without equality is just privilege and injustice.**

#### Propaganda by the deed
- Bakunin disagreed with Proudhon that a violent revolution was unnecessary. He saw in his day that the state would not let go of power, and capitalism was now deeply entrenched in society.
- He was committed to extreme direct action: 'cells' doing propaganda of the deed — like mass strikes, refusing conscription, tax evasion, rent evasion, debt evasion, etc.
	- the aim of these actions is to empower the masses to reach class consciousness, and then free themselves
- it wouldn't be a top-down revolution, though — because those encourage a new state.
