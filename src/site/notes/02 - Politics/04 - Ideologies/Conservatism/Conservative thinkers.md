## Thomas Hobbes
Hobbes wrote 'Leviathan'. His work is very easy to understand if you understand it as 'Locke, but he hates everyone.'

### Order
Hobbes believed that the natural state of nature was of war, and without the state, this would be the natural end point. Therefore, an authoritarian state is needed to prevent the collapse of social order.

> [!quote] Quotes
> 'How could a state be governed, or protected... if every individual remained free to obey or not obey the law according to his private opinion?'
> 
> Humans live in 'continual fear, and danger of violent death' and what life they have is 'solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short.'

### Social contract
Hobbes is also a social contract theorist, but his version is a bit different to liberal social contract theorists.

He agreed that the contract between ruler and ruled was contractual, but thought that once one accepted sovereign power, they were accepting authoritarian rule: trading liberty for safety.

People are bound by the contract so long as the sovereign protects their subjects. However, if they fail and people think that their condition is no better than that of the free-for-all outside the state, they then have the right to disobey sovereign authority.

### Human nature
Hobbes has a very pessimistic view of human nature. He thinks that humans aren't capable of restraint, and even the moderate would turn to violence to protect themselves and their belongings: a.k.a. humans are naturally competitive and aggressive.

They are also very bad at understanding the world around them, as capacity for reason is fragile. As a result, people are narcissistic and selfish.

As a result, Hobbes believed war came more naturally to humans than order. To life in order requires humans to supress their natural instincts and allow rule over them. The best people can hope for is a peaceful life under a sovereign authority.

> [!quote] Humans are in 'a perpetual and restless desire for power after power that ceases only in death.'

## Edmund Burke
Burke's major work is 'Reflections on the Revolution in France.' He opposed the revolution's commitments to abstract principles of the 'universal rights of man' and argued that wisdom lied in experience, tradition, and history.

### Change
Burke argued that political change should be made with caution, and based on reality — not abstract speculation. One should accept that society is too complex to be fully understood and that the consequences of radical change cannot be predicted.

> [!quote] 'Very plausible schemes, with very pleasing commencements, have often shameful and lamentable conclusions.'

This doesn't mean we should never seen change, but that we should do it carefully and prudently.

His criticisms of the French Revolution were that ideological purity and aspirations were without understanding of how to create a new society. Burke rejected the idea that humans were capable of replacing one set of rules with another, because we lack the capacity to fully understand the world.

### Tradition
Burke loved tradition. He argued that:
> [!quote] *'Society is indeed a contract',* but that partners of this contract are *'those who are living, those who are dead, and those who are yet to be born.'*

The present generation don't know better than previous. Rights can only be understood within historical context.

According to Burke, respect for tradition promotes continuity and stability. It also establishes a duty for each generation to protect and pass on tradition to the next.

### Organicism, hierarchy, and paternalism
Burke loved organicism: believing society was naturally hierarchic and interconnected. He argued that humans need to feel connected to others to feel safe and flourish — identifying family, church, and local community as key groups. 

> [!quote] We should *'learn to love the little platoons in society to which we belong.'*

He also argued that only the aristocracy, who were *'born to rule'*, had the wisdom to govern, as other classes could not help but put their own narrow interests first. 

During his time as MP of Bristol, he expressed this in a speech (which promptly got him unelected next term.)

> [!quote] 'Your representative owes you, not his industry only, but his judgement; and he betrays, instead of serving you, if he sacrifices it to your opinion.'



## Michael Oakeshott
Oakeshott's best known works include 'Rationalism in Politics' and 'On human conduct'.

### Human imperfection
He suggested the world is unknowable and unpredictable, hence beyond the grasp of humans.

Instead of creating a new society with untested ideas, political leaders should *'keep afloat on an even keel.* The world is unpredictable, and the only option is to keep the ship afloat. Attempts to radically change direction will lead to capsizing.

In his essay 'On Being Conservative', Oakeshott explained what he believed to be the conservative disposition:

> [!quote] ‘To be conservative … is to prefer the familiar to the unknown, to prefer the tried to the untried, fact to mystery, the actual to the possible, the limited to the unbounded, the near to the distant, the sufficient to the superabundant, the convenient to the perfect, present laughter to utopian bliss.’

### Anti-rational

The view (rationalism) that 'correct' political decisions can be made by simply applying the rules and calculating the consequences is wrong. Politics should be '*a conversation, not an argument*' — decisions should be made with consensus and be of practical, tested solutions — not ideological nonsense.

The world is complicated, and one should be suspicious of leaders who claim that they can 'pilot' the ship to a specific destination, as this is unlikely. Parliament did not develop on the basis of rationalism or ideology, but over centuries in response to circumstances.

His view of rationalism is linked to his view of human nature. Oakeshott distrusted rational arguments, preferring traditional values and established customs based on the above conservative disposition. Politics can only be successfully conducted if it accommodates existing traditions and practices.



## Ayn Rand
Born in the USSR, she emigrated to the US when she was 21. Her works include 'The Fountainhead' and 'Atlas Shrugged', a portrait of a dystopian USA.

### Objectivism
Objectivism is a philosophy of rational individualism, where there is no greater moral goal than achieving one's happiness. She called it '*a practical philosophy for living on earth*' — arguing that reason and rationality are the only things that one can be sure of.

In 'Atlas Shrugged' (1957), Rand showed that without the efforts of rational and productive people, the economy would collapse and so would society. Rand described the theme of the novel as '*the role of the mind in man's existence... and the demonstration of a new moral philosophy: the morality of rational self-interest.*' This is rational egoism. She said that the individual should '*exist for his own sake, neither sacrificing himself to others nor sacrificing others to himself*.' and Egoism being '*the virtue of selfishness*'. Altruism, for Rand, was incompatible with human happiness.

She also argued that conforming to the norms of society hindered human's ability to be free ('*a man has rights, which neither God nor King nor other men can take from him*') She is arguing that each society broke out of one form of conformity into another, each one enslaving humans in different ways. The only way to be truly free is to break from this and live as one sees fit.

She considered reason to be single most significant part of her philosophy. '*I am not an advocate of egoism, but of reason. If one recognises the supremacy of reason and applies it consistently, the rest all follows.*'

### Freedom
Rand defended laissez-faire capitalism on the grounds that it guarantees freedom and provides for the emergence of the elites needed to govern society. Objectivism favours a political and economic system consistent with both individualism and laissez-faire capitalism.

Her novel 'Atlas Shrugged' showed state intervention paralysing businesses. The heroes of her book are entrepreneurs and industrialists, whose productivity underpins society. 

> [!quote] 'every man is an end in himself, not the means to the ends of others.'

She believed that any attempt to control other's actions undermined one's ability to work freely and productively.



## Robert Nozick
Nozick, an American political philosopher, developed a form of rights-based libertarianism in response to John Rawls. His major work, 'Anarchy, state, and utopia' rejects welfare and redistribution, and advances the case for minimum government and minimal taxation.

### Libertarianism
Nozick argued that any state that isn't as small as possible is incompatible with individual rights — only a minimal state is morally justified. He specifically means a state which ensured '*protection against force, theft, fraud, enforcement of contracts and so on*', a.k.a. a nightwatchman state.

Nozick echoes Locke's theory of property ownership: everyone has the right to claim the products of their own labour as their property, and this includes doing whatever they wish with it, provided that this does not violate anyone else's rights. Everyone also has the right to punish those who violate or attempt to violate one's natural rights.

### Taxation and entitlement theory
The most famous conclusion of Nozick's book was that taxation was morally indefensible: it is '*legalised slavery*', where a person is forced to work towards another's gain. He argued that any compulsory taxes used to benefit others is unjust, because it is 'forced labour'.

Echoing Kant (as does Rand), he argued that individuals own their bodies, talents, abilities and labour, and are therefore an '*end in themselves*'. Humans are therefore entitled to do with their property what they wish.

Nozick hence argued for 'entitlement theory', a direct counter to Rawl's distributive justice. According to him, anyone who acquired property through just means was morally entitled to it. He included three ways to do this (calling property 'holdings')
1. if something is unowned, where the acquisition would not disadvantage others
2. the voluntary transfer of ownership to someone else
3. the transfer of ownership to rectify past injustices
