## Summary
-> Christianity is the normative means of salvation, but anonymous Christians or those of other religious faiths can still access the same benefits of Jesus' death on the cross. Often seen as the middle ground between exclusivism (which many Christians see as contradictory to the idea of a benevolent God) and Pluralism (which runs the risk of devaluing the objective truths that Christianity puts forward.)

## Structural inclusivism
### Karl Rahner
- any religion whose structures develop an openness to God's grace revealed in Christ may receive God's salvation.

Rahner specifically developed an inclusivist understanding of Christianity, with Christ retaining his position as mediation of the divine, while acknowledging that faith in him may not be the only way to salvation.

Rahner still adheres to the "*Solas Christus*" principle, arguing that it was Jesus' atonement that allows people to be saved though God's grace. however, he argues that other religions can mediate this grace.

### Restrictive inclusivism
-> non-Christian religion is only good as preparation for salvation, it is not salvific along
- Christianity is the one true religion (because of Jesus' relationship with God) however those before Jesus, such as the figures of the Old Testament, must have been able to be saved
- Equally, other religions can guide people to salvation, although only until they encounter the true Christian message — yet we must not assume that non-Christian religions have no knowledge of the truth

### Gavin D'Costa and the Holy Spirit
- Seeing only Christ as the path to salvation (*Christomonism*) is misleading and ignores the role of the Holy Spirit.
- Salvation has to be approached in the *Trinitarian manner* instead, if a means of inclusivist doctrine is to be formed
- -> beyond the figure of Jesus, the Holy Spirit may well reveal itself through other means, and so other religions may possess truths that Christianity can learn from.
- This avoids the issue with Rahner's 'anonymous Christians' and opens other religions to possess their own truths that connects to, but are different from, Christianity 
- -> although, is this not pluralism?

## Critiques of Inclusivism
1. Does inclusivism overcome the issue of the postcode lottery?
2. Does it diminish Christ's role in salvation?

A. Still doesn't overcome it — morals are significantly changed by the society that we live in, enough that expecting people to universally fulfil the specific moral requirements of Christianity is not only unrealistic, but it is just a more subtle version of the postcode lottery.

B. Yes, it does diminish Christ's role in salvation. Even though the whole point of the sacrifice and resurrection is that it saves everyone, the steps to actually receiving salvation includes accepting Christ, which means that if one believes that you can receive grace without Christ, that does somewhat diminish it.


### Evaluating
#### Pros
- seems to find a balance between the uniqueness of Christ and the loving God that wishes to save all
- acknowledges the idea of sensus divinatus/natural theology — that we all have an innate idea of God, no matter if we identify as Christian 
- Judgement is based on action and not belief, as shown in the Parable of the Sheep and the Goats.

#### Cons
- Patronizing to other faiths, in the way that it sees their search for the truth as inferior to the Christian truth — their role seems ancillary insofar as they merely offer truth until one uncovers the ultimate truth in Christianity
	- debatably western-centric
- Rahner's inclusivism collapses into exclusivism as it fundamentally asserts that the only knowable path to salvation is Christianity, with other religions simply conforming to Grace mediated by Christ
	- -> other religions not recognised for the truths they present independently of Christ and are simply regarded as unwitting extensions of Christ's grace
- as the same time as belittling other religions, it undermines Christianity by de-centralising the Christ event, in the case that other religions can seek truth too.
	- -> it reduces Christianity to a matter of living well.
- the *fides ex auditu* principle is clearly not possible for anonymous Christians — how are they able to fully confess their sins, repent and seek amendment of life? D'Costa considers this a fundamental condition of being Christian, how can someone implicitly confess their sins in a way that is recognisable Christian, without being so?
- the notion of 'anonymous Christians' is incoherent -> to be a member of religion involves consent
- Misused the notion of *votum ecclesia* — phrase refers to those who consciously wish to become members of the Christian Church, it does not make sense to implicitly wish to become a member of something without knowing what it is.

**By appealing to both Christians and non-Christians it seems to satisfy neither, by belittling other religions and decentralising Jesus from salvation.**
