## Intro
- With Hick's publication of 'God and the Universe of Faiths' in 1973, pluralism in Christianity has been discussed in greater depth and adopted widely among many theologians
- many critics have accused pluralistic beliefs of being little more than heresy and the result of modern, secular ideas masquerading within the boundaries of Christian faith
- Hick argues however, that pluralism is not relativism — religion is not final; as all are aiming towards salvation they are in a process of growth and evolution thus while disagreements exist they cannot be complete. If all are pointing to the same source of salvation, God, then a religion with full understanding would have no internal or external conflicts

## Universal theological pluralism - John Hick
### The real
- the grounds for religious belief is religious experience — there is no way to discern between the truth-competing religious experiences, which are often private and ineffable
- differences between experiences are as a result of cultural or upbringing differences
- Hick contends that all these experiences originate from the same source, God or 'the Real' — he rejects the notion that religious experience is a projection, as the ultimate cause is 'the real'

### Kant's epistemology
- due to differences in culture or situation, the Noumena of the divine (the Real) is experiences through different Phenomena (religions); neither is more or less truthful, illustrated by Wittgenstein's duck-rabbit picture
- reality comes as a result of different conceptual schemes which lead to differing interpretations of the same truths

### Reality centred
- John Hick's description of the spiritual or religious live focused on the Real. A reality centred life is unselfish and concerned for the welfare of others; Hick argues that all authentic religions are those which uphold the categorical imperative and treat one's neighbours as one treats oneself.

### Global theory — Copernican Revolution
- Copernican revolution which shifts theology away from having a Christological bias, which often too means Christianity only examines other religions in the light of Christian concepts
- there is no rational basis to claim Christianity's accuracy, such an approach could only improve dialogue and relations between religions

### Demythologising Christ
- the idea of demythologising goes back to C20th New Testament scholar Rudolf Bultmann; believed the gospels contained essential truths expressed through myths — in order to understand the core message of the gospels it is essential to demythologise the stories around Christ, such as the Trinity of the Incarnation
- recognising Jesus' humanity is the most important aspect, making him more morally inspiring than if he were divine -> the term 'son of God' was widely used to describe people who behaved like God in some way.
	- it was only when Christianity moved into the gentile world that it began to be taken literally rather than metaphorically.
- Jesus is not unique in Hick's eyes, and the arguments that he is the Son of God and only path to salvation are ultimately unwarranted

### Theological task
#### Theocentric not Christocentric
-> revelation emanates from God not Christ, reality centred, not Christ centred. The task of theologians is to reinterpret the doctrines of the incarnation, atonement and resurrection as myths not facts

#### The myth of Jesus' divinity
-> Jesus is supposedly uniquely the incarnate son of God, but Hick provides a number of reasons why the *sola Christus* should be abandoned. Incarnation was originally a myth to explain Jesus' consciousness of God

#### Jesus as a gift to the world
-> For Christianity to adapt to the modern world it must rid itself of the ancient doctrines laid down at the great councils of Nicea and Chalcedon which defined the divinity of Christ and established the idea of the Trinity. Once Christianity rids itself of these exclusive doctrines, then Jesus can be appropriated by other religions who see him in a man who can enlarge the relationship with God to which they have already come within their own tradition. Jesus' social teaching and example is therefore a gift to the world just as other great religious leaders contributed to the world.

## Pluriform theological pluralism
The difference between UTP and PTP is that UTP believes there is one underlying Real, whereas PTP believes that there are many Reals experienced by each religion.

No one can have knowledge of the Real so each religion has its own version of salvation, liberation, and knowledge.

Keith Ward believes we may have differing beliefs but reasons for them can be equally valid.

Truth is found through religious experience, which changes in each religion.

## Ethical theological pluralism
- Paul Knitter believes all religions have the same soteriological aim of liberating humans from suffering, injustice, intolerance and falsehood; religions are 'soteriocentric' and not reality-centric
- inspired by liberation theology — primary purpose of a religious community is praxis: action against injustice
- purpose of theology is to provide the means whereby the different religions can share and discuss their differing perspectives of justice according to their belief systems; differences = creative and fruitful dialogue
- dialogue will enhance and develop each religions distinctive interpretation of reality — all that is required is that different religions are open to new interpretations and share their common desire for a better world

## Evaluation of Pluralism
### Pros
- it's attractiveness lies in its desire to develop the secular liberal aim that successful societies are ones where people of different beliefs coexist.
- Recent religiously motivated events such as the London Suicide Bombings in 2005, or the Charlie Hebdo shootings in Paris 2015, have fuelled theological pluralist's desire to remove any imperialist motives from religions claiming to be superior to one another.
- Non-realists would similarly argue that there is nothing to suggest that 'the Real' is in the noumena whatsoever — rather everything is simply phenomena and religious experience is simple projection; the process of demythologising should extent to God completely as there is no ultimate reality or God

### Cons
- undermines the *sola Christus* principle — is it even Christian if it avoids beliefs about Christ and his revelation of God...
- Conservative Christians argue that Hick's demythologising devalues the Bible and removes its eschatological significance, especially with his denial of the resurrection
- Hick's UTP is a form of exclusivism — it claims that it knows what 'the Real' is and judges other religions by this
- Hick build on Kant's epistemology — why is this more superior than other truth claims?
- UTP presupposes that all religions have a sense of 'the Real', but many forms of Buddhism reject such a notion; other religions think their deity is the ultimate — there is no real beyond it.
- ETP suggests religions are only interested in praxis and social liberation, but political liberation is not necessarily religious?
- By suggesting that all religious claims are myths and not to be treated objectively, pluralism destroys what religious people actually believe themselves — Global theology has no doctrine or content, not really a religion
	- -> (D'Costa) or does it allow multiple truths? Think Wittgenstein's language games
