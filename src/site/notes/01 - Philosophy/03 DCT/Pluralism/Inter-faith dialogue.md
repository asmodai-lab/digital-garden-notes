##### Dabru Emet
Dabru Emet (speak the truth) was published in 2000. It was a Jewish statement in Christians and Christianity, and meant to be interfaith dialogue.

It was signed by over 150 Rabbis/Jewish scholars from the USA, Canada, the UK and Israel.

It calls for Jewish people to learn about the efforts of Christians to honour Judaism and to reflect on what Judaism might say about Christianity.

This was a significant move in the light of antisemitism which culminated in the holocaust.

- it looked for common ground.
	1. they share the same God
	2. both accept the authority of scripture
	3. both need to work for peace
	4. both have been affected by the large Jewish contribution to culture & economics
- discussion followed, which said that co-operation between the two religions should be an everyday occurrence, and that they should continue to work together and communicate.

It did challenge individual Christians — to an extent — but many might consider it's main achievement is for Christianity as an institution to rethink:
- it's teachings/doctrines
- how it reads scripture
- the nature and language of its worship
- it's education policies
- it's church policies

> [!tip] This is an example of structural inclusivism.

##### A common word between us and you
Published in 2007, it was a letter from 138 Muslim scholars and leaders to the leaders of Christian churches.

It's title is based on Qur'an verse 3:64:

> [!example] 3:64
> 'People of the Book! Come to a word common between us and you: that we shall serve non but Allah and shall associate none with Him in His divinity and that some of us will not take others as lords beside Allah. And if they turn their backs (from accepting this call), tell them: "Bear witness that we are the ones who have submitted ourselves exclusively to Allah." '

-> the common word is the love of God and neighbour in this quest for peace and justice.

- 'A common word between us and you' recognised that there are differences between religions, and that both should study their own faith to see what the demands of their religion makes to its adherents.
- It looked at the central message of both scriptures, and the responsibilities both have: to work for everyone's good.
- it looked at the need for both of them to live up to their teaching.
- it said that both must continue to find ways to engage with each other on a regular basis.

It generated many discussions, meetings and publications. One of the largest meetings was convened by Rowan Williams (whilst he was Archbishop of Canterbury).
- 50 Church leaders and scholars from around the world met and produced a letter by way of response called "A common word for the Good."
	- the dialogue in the two letters represents how inter-faith work can operate internationally at an institutional level.

> [!tip] This is also an example of structural inclusivism.

### "Sharing the Gospel of salvation"
- this is a Church of England document.

The background is that a question was debated at the Synod 2006 by Paul Eddy:

> [!question] "Should the Church of England be trying to convert people of other faiths to Christianity?"
> They needed to have a statement about what Christians should do about claiming salvation in Christ, particularly in areas with high religious diversity.
> 
> Christians became very fearful about causing offence, yet the teaching of the Church from Jesus is:
> >[!quote] "Go and make disciples of all nations."

#### The debate
The debate should be viewed in the light of these points:
1. Christian message: God's plan to save the world, uniquely achieved through Jesus Christ
2. The Church must witness to this, and as a Christian disciple, individuals will share their faith with others
> [!quote] The Great Commission (Matthew 28)
> "Go and make disciples of all nations"/"make disciples of all men"
> 

^ This is how Christianity came to Britain, historically.

#### The dangers
In history, we see:
1. how Christianity got caught up in politics
2. how Christians have been culturally insensitive
3. how they haven't lived up to the teachings of the Bible
4. how they've treated Mission like a marketing exercise, and non-Christians as targets, not people
> [!quote] "Proclamation is not the same as selling a product in the marketplace; it means to tell people what you think is good about what you believe and practice (so, this is not incompatible with inter-faith dialogue)."
> In other words, if a mission means to proclaim a person's beliefs as a Christian then this should be an important reason for engaging in inter-faith dialogue.

5. Sharing in faith the right/wrong way (particularly in regards to the uniqueness of Christ.)

#### The conclusions
- dialogue should re-affirm belief in the Oneness of God
	- and that Jesus came to earth as God incarnate
- God offers salvation to all even if people do not know
- Christians should bring people to faith in Jesus explicitly and baptise them into the Church
- Christians in areas where other religions are dominant should go beyond just tolerance but make an effort to engage and find opportunities to talk

##### Good practice
- learn from the experience of those in diverse areas
- always treat others as you'd wish to be treated
- be sensitive when talking to others
- remember that nobody wants to be pressured into changing their views
- remember that it can be difficult for some to change their views, due to the possibility of ostracisation from their family and community.
