## Overview
Women tend to appear in 'side character' roles to a story focussed on a man. Obviously, this tells us something about the role of women in the Biblical world.

It's important to remember that the Bible is many different books put together at different times, drawing on a variety of literary strands and traditions. Consequently, there isn't really one, singular 'Biblical view of women', but a bubbling pot of different teachings.

Furthermore, someone like Paul did not set out to 'explain his views on women', he was writing letters to help communities with specific problems: i.e. pastoral advice, not theology. If we are to deduce his attitude to women, there's a fair bit of reading between the lines.

With all this in mind, it is still possible to make some generalisations. For example, it's fair to say that, generally, women in the Old Testament are often meek and virtuous, **or** sexual temptresses. There are, however, women who don't fit these stereotypes (e.g. Deborah). Again, it's fair to say that — generally — the Old Testament reflected patriarchal attitudes, and Jesus' teachings were more inclusive. But, not all texts fit that pattern.

It's easier to say that *some* Biblical texts have been used and interpreted to justify sexist attitudes. The following have all been justified on the basis of the Bible;
- men should have authority over women, and wives should obey their husbands
- a woman's primary role is to procreate and raise children
- women are more prone to sin
- women cannot be priests
- women should be sexually pure
- women should dress modestly
- men can divorce their wives, but women cannot divorce their husbands
- women (and men) should not use contraception
- rape of women is less bad then rape of men

**Elizabeth Cady Stanton** (a liberal feminist) said that as long as the Bible has influence over minds, it will be important for feminists to engage with it.

If you want to investigate further, go to https://womeninthebible.net and have a look.

## Women in the Old Testament

### Victims
> These are women who are victims of violence, and not in control of their own fate.

#### Hagar
Hagar was Sarah's Egyptian slave. Sarah told Abram (her husband) to take Hagar as a mistress in order to get the kids God promised him, because Sarah was implied to be infertile. And, since Sarah owned Hagar, Hagar's children would also be owned by Sarah.

Abram did as Sarah suggested, but when Hagar *"knew she was pregnant, she began to despise her mistress."* Sarah mistreated Hagar until she ran away. 

Hagar later returned, but this was after Sarah had birthed a son — so she asked Abram (now called Abraham) to dismiss Hagar. Abraham asked God for advice and God told him to do as his wife said.

So, Abraham gave Hagar water and sent her to the desert. The water ran out and: '*when the water in the skin was gone, she put the boy under one of the bushes. Then she went off and sat down about a bowshot away, for she thought "I cannot watch the boy die." And as she sat there, she began to sob.*'

Hagar then had a vision of God and found a spring, where both she and Ishmael survived, thanks to God's intervention.

Hagar had no choice in her fate and was used by Sarah and Abraham. Sarah was also arguably a victim — being childless in a time where a woman's worth was predicated on her ability to bear children.

#### Jephthah's daughter

Jephthah rashly promised to sacrifice the first thing he found when he got home, if God would assure him military victory. He then won a battle.

Alas, the first thing he found when he got home was his daughter, also his only child. He said; "*Alas, my daughter! You have brought me very low, and you have become the cause of great trouble for me; for I have opened my mouth to the Lord, and cannot take back my vow*". 

She tells him to keep his vow, and he sacrificed her, after allowing her a couple months in the mountains to mourn the fact that she will never marry.

### Dangerous/temptress women
#### Eve
Eve is portrayed as being weak to sin and blamed for leading Adam into it. Her punishment included having a man as 'master' over her.

This story is often used to demonstrate the dangers of women taking charge, because women are more vulnerable to sin/temptation.

However, some feminists (like Phyllis Trible) have reclaimed the story and highlight toe appeal of Eve as the one who seeks out knowledge and takes initiative.

#### Delilah and Judith
Delilah was loved by the Israelite strongman hero Samson. The Philistines (the Israelite's enemies) wanted her to find out the secret of Samson's strength. At first he gives her false answers. She said *'How can you say 'I love you' when your heart is not with me? You have mocked me three times now and have not told me what makes your strength so great.'* 

He then told her the secret of his strength. Delilah passed this on to the Philistines and Samson had his hair cut off and his eyes gouged out.

In a somewhat similar story an Israelite named **Judith** made herself beautify and met the Persian general. He wanted to seduce her but she waited until he fell asleep drunk and cut of his head. She took the severed head back to the town and the Persian army scattered when they saw that they had lost their leader. As Judith was an Israelite she was hailed as a heroine (whereas Delilah is portrayed as a baddie!)

### Virtuous women
#### Esther
Esther is celebrated as a heroine within Judaism for saving the Jewish people from death.

Esther was a Jewish orphan chosen to be part of King Douchebag's harem. According to the story: '*The king shall appoint officers in all the provinces of his kingdom, and they shall select beautiful young virgins to be brought to the harem in Susa. Let them be entrusted to the king's eunuch who is in charge of the women, and let ointments and whatever else they need to be given to them.*'

So, the girls would then have a year to 'beautify' themselves, before being sent to the king, who would then chose his favourite to be his wife. He chose Esther, but he didn't know she was Jewish.

Esther's uncle, a servant of the king, offended some official dude. In retaliation, the official resolved to kill him and all the Jews. Esther, hearing this, prayed to God for guidance — and after several days in prayer, she 'beautified' herself, went to the king, and pleaded with him to spare the Jewish people. 

She succeeded, and the king spared the Jews and punished the official.

The Jewish festival of Purim celebrates this story: a vulnerable orphan in a foreign land, making the most of her opportunities, becoming queen and using her position to save others.

#### Ruth
Ruth is another vulnerable person who acted wisely on her opportunities — celebrated as an example of loyalty and virtue.

Ruth and her mother in law Naomi were both widowed, and Naomi had decided to travel back to her homeland. She told Ruth to also go home to her own family, but Ruth refused, saying: "*Where you go, I will go, and where you stay I will stay. Your people will be my people and your God my God*."

Boaz, a relation of Naomi (and therefore also a relation of Ruth's late husband) was a rich land owner. Jewish laws instructed land owners to leave the very edge of their crops for the poor, called 'gleaning'. Boaz spotted Ruth gleaning in his fields and told his workers to be kind to her.

### Powerful women
#### Deborah
Deborah was a priestess and Judge at the time when the Judges ruled Israel. 

Judges 4 tells the story of her military victory over the Philistines. She told the Israelite commander how to trick them into driving their chariots over boggy ground so that they would sink and get stuck. When Deborah first outlined her plan to the commander, he said '*If you go with me, I will go; but if you don't go with me, I won't go.*'

#### Miriam
Miriam was Moses' older sister, and helps to save him when the Pharaoh insisted that all boys born to Hebrew slaves be killed at birth. Later on, she is described as a prophet in their own right.
