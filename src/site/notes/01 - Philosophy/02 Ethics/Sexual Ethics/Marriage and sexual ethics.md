### Theology of the body

1. **Original Solitude**

Before encountering Eve, Adam experiences himself as different from the animals. He is bodily, yet capable of self-knowledge and moral choice. This establishes the idea of the human person as a unity of body and soul, and not reducible to biology.

2. **Original Unity**

This is the *key idea.*

When Adam meets Eve, he recognises her not as a rival or an object but as “bone of my bones, flesh of my flesh.” John Paul II argues that this moment reveals:

- A reciprocal self-gift
- A unity that is physical and spiritual
- A recognition of shared dignity
- The grounding of the “one flesh” union as a sign of God’s love

> Original unity therefore precedes sin; it is how the human body was meant to mean before distortion.

3. **Nakedness Without Shame**

In Eden, nakedness symbolises transparency. The body reveals the person truly. Sexual desire is not possessive but oriented toward communion. Shame enters only after the Fall, when the meaning of the body becomes vulnerable to misuse.

4. **The Echo of the Garden**

Even though original unity is lost, we still feel its echo. Human longing for intimacy, fidelity, and stable self-giving relationships points back to a primordial truth: we are meant for communion.

John Paul II calls this the “nuptial meaning of the body.” The body expresses a vocation to love, and sexual ethics only make sense within that understanding.

#### Why This Matters for Ethics

This way of thinking underpins:

- Catholic teaching on marriage
- Opposition to purely utilitarian accounts of sex
- The idea that the body has inherent meaning, not merely socially constructed meaning
- The claim that sexual acts speak a moral language

In other words, **sex is not just an action—it is a sign of the person you are becoming.**

### Humanae Vitae

1. **Vision of the Human Person**

>Paul VI insists that marriage is a communion of persons marked by fidelity, self-giving love, and openness to life.
>>He rejects any view that reduces sex to pleasure, power, or personal fulfilment without responsibility.

2. **The Two Meanings of the Sexual Act**

*This is the hinge of the whole encyclical:*

Unitive, expressing love and self-gift Procreative – openness to the creation of life

Paul VI’s argument is that these two meanings are not separable without violating the integrity of the person.

3. **Artificial Contraception**

The encyclical famously reaffirms the prohibition on artificial contraception. Its logic:

- Sexual acts carry an inherent moral structure.
- To deliberately close the procreative meaning is to falsify the act.
- Natural Family Planning is permitted because it cooperates with, rather than blocks, the natural rhythms of fertility.

4. **Predictions (often cited in OCR essays)**

Paul VI warned that widespread contraceptive use might lead to:

- A lowering of moral standards
- Increased objectification of women
- Governments or institutions exerting control over reproduction
- A general cultural separation between sex and responsibility

> Even secular commentators note that elements of these predictions have resonance in debates about consent culture, pornography, and bioethics.
> >And I personally think that it is Bullshit with a capital B.

5. **The Theological Frame**

Humanae Vitae sits downstream from the same theological river as the Anglican Preface and the Theology of the Body, even though the traditions differ:

- Marriage is a vocation shaped by creation
- The body is not morally neutral
- Sexual acts ought to express both unity and openness to life
- Human flourishing is linked to living in harmony with the moral order
