The way you write source questions is almost the same as writing a normal 30 marker. There are only two things you need to do differently:
1. stick to the themes provided in the question
2. use a quote from it every now and then

Other than that? Write it exactly the same as you write a 30 marker:
- use your own examples
- follow the paragraph structure
- analyse, evaluate, etc

You can practically ignore the source after you've done the two things above.

Anyway, please note that this framing is something I use *to tackle my specific issues with essay writing*. I imagine that it will still be helpful, but please keep in mind that you should tailor this to which issues you face. And, to be specific, here are the issues I face:
- timing/writing too much
- having insubstantial analysis
- not explaining things *enough* (or at all)
- weak evaluation
- not giving the weak point enough time to shine

How do I solve these?
1. timing & not giving the weak point enough time to shine.
Sticking STRONGLY to the essay structure
2. insubstantial analysis/explanation/evaluation
Asking myself a series of questions as I go through the essay in order to stay on topic and have strong arguments.

To show how I do this, I'll do a walkthrough of how I'd write this question.

And now, a walkthrough of a paragraph. 

**A weak point is that we 'can and do' 'regularly remove governments', so the UK doesn't have a democratic deficit.**
- this is the only point (i.e. when you are stating your point) where you *need* to use quotes, when doing a source question. It would be great to use more elsewhere, but as long as you do it here (and in your counter-point!), you probably aren't going to get capped on marks for not quoting.
- Also, KEEP YOUR QUOTES SHORT. Frankly, what I was doing there was too long. 3-5 words, MAX.
- remember to link back to the question here! You might forget in the rest of the paragraph, but as long as you are clearly doing it at the start and the end ('so the UK doesn't have a democratic deficit' — I'M USING WORDS FROM THE QUESTION) you should be fine, as long as you didn't go on a completely unrelated tangent midway through

**For example, Kier Starmer's Labour was elected in the 2024 General Election, removing the Conservatives from government.**
- USE THE MOST OBVIOUS EXAMPLE. this means
	- use one from the past 5 years (if you can, past year)
	- use the big one (i.e. a general election)
- you can include your minor/older examples, but do it LATER. They're great, and good to include for quickly showing that you know a lot about a topic (even if you don't), but your main example *should be the obvious one.*
- note, I could have talked about the Gorton&Denton by-election here, or if I was writing this in my A-Levels, the Welsh Sennedd elections would also be better examples. I'm not using the former here because it would be better served in the participation theme, and the latter because it hasn't happened yet.

**This happened because of deep dissatisfaction with the Conservative's record in government, such as from party-gate, or Truss' 50 days in government.**
- when doing analysis, a common problem is that you restate your point. I could've easily said 'this shows that we do regularly remove governments, so therefore there isn't a democratic deficit'. But that ISN'T ANALYSIS. It's boring, and says nothing — but it's very easy to do. So, how do we avoid this? The question asking method!
	- ask yourself one of two questions: 'What caused this?', or 'What did it cause?' This, more often then not, strengthens your later evaluation: because it's very easy to point out that something *wasn't* caused/didn't cause xyz. 
- Here, it was 'what caused Labour to be elected?' Oh, look, it's dissatisfaction with the conservatives!
- and, since I want to set something up for later, I'll add more evidence:

**This is further demonstrated by Starmer getting less of the vote than Corbyn did in the 2019 General Election: showing that it was not Labour's popularity that won the General Election, but the Conservative's unpopularity.**

- now is time for your old examples! This quickly shows a) depth of knowledge (good for marks!) and b) that this is not a one-off event.
**A similar thing occurred in the 2010 and 1997 General Elections.**

- and now, we've given the opposing point a proper leg to stand on. Now, it's all the more satisfying when we break it in half :)

**A stronger point is that UK democracy has a 'problem with legitimacy' with our elected governments.**
- again, remember to quote here! If nowhere else, quote in your point sentence.

**For example, in the 2024 General Election, Labour got ~30% of the vote, but ~60% of the seats. Additionally, the Lib-Dems got 12% of the vote and 72 seats, yet Reform UK got 14% of the vote and 5 seats.**

- you now see what I was setting up earlier?

**In fact, no government since the 1950's has been elected on more than 50% of the vote (despite forming a government requiring 50% of the seats) and none in the last 3 decades have been elected on more than 45% of the vote.**
- again, this is your extra evidence.

- Now, here it would be easy to just say 'this shows a clear problem with legitimacy in our governments, when their power does not correlate to the percentage of the vote they received', BUT THIS ISN'T ANALYSIS. Remember: what causes this/what is caused by this?

**This demonstrates that First Past The Post gives an unfair 'winner's bonus', which then causes the makeup of parliament to be disproportionate when compared to the popular vote.**
- see, linking to electoral systems! If you wanted, you could add a quote from the source, 'proportional representation.'
- also, note that there are several other things I could talk about here. I could counter with constituency-MP link, or the 'representatives' Burkean concept, or even the 'strong government' argument. However, YOU ONLY HAVE 40 MINUTES PER ESSAY. THERE IS NOT ENOUGH GODDAMN TIME. You don't need to include everything in a politics essay: *that isn't the point.* The point is TO MAKE A GOOD ARGUMENT. And, especially since this is a source question, YOU CAN IGNORE THE POINTS THAT ARE INCONVENIENT TO YOUR ESSAY. The way to get a good mark isn't to make the most niche points, or use the most out-there evidence: it's making the most obvious points in the clearest way possible with a reasoned conclusion from it. 
- Who gives a crap about Thatcher's cabinet? Or John Major and Black Wednesday, or the abysmal turnout in the 2005 GE? USE THE MOST RECENT EXAMPLE. MOST OF THE TIME, IT'S ALSO THE MOST OBVIOUS ONE. JUST DO IT. IT'S SO MUCH BETTER FOR YOUR ARGUMENT.

And now, evaluation. There is two main ways to go about this: poking holes in either the weak point's *evidence*, or it's *analysis.* Here, we come back to the multiple questions method. First, if poking holes in the evidence:
- is this a one-off example? Does it happen frequently?
-> E.g. you use Gorton & Denton to show that turnout in smaller elections can be high in your main point, when it's actually an outlier.
- is it irrelevant to the answer?
-> If you only have old examples for a point, then it's irrelevant to the current day. Like if you used the example of King Charles I to show that monarchs can take away rights — that happened in the 1700s (I think). Who cares. Nobody.

And, to poke holes in the analysis:
- is there a different cause?
-> 'Labour got elected because they are popular!': when they got less of the popular vote than Corbyn
-> 'Labour got elected because they are popular!': actually, in ~1/3 of the seats they won in 2024, it was won by Reform splitting the right-wing vote from the Conservatives. If we assume that Reform voters would have all voted Conservative without Reform, then Labour would have gotten almost the exact number of seats they won in 2019, off by about 3

- does it not show the whole picture? (i.e. what does it cause)
-> 'Johnson got kicked out by public opinion': no, it was his cabinet. Yes, because they feared public opinion/backlash, but Johnson was unpopular for MONTHS before he got forced to resign.
-> 'Starmer's U-turns are due to public backlash': his majority is so big that it takes a LOT of his MP's to actually cause a U-turn. The Online Safety Act has been his most unpopular policy for months (it got a petition against it with 6 million signatures) and he has stuck by it, even continuing it with the Child and Welfare Bill. What about digital ID? or farmer's inheritance tax? 

**Overall, it's clear that UK elections do not elect legitimate governments: and so it does not matter if the electorate 'can' remove a government, because even if they manage: the one they elect in place almost always has power disproportionate to it's actual mandate because of the FPTP voting system: therefore, no matter if the government can change, it is systematically illegitimate. Therefore, the UK has a systematic democratic deficit to a large extent.**
- link back to the question!
- Why is the weaker point weak? Well, refer to the above questions to answer that. However, remember to point out why the stronger point is strong.
	- here, I'm using the irrelevancy argument: I don't care if we can remove the government, because any government elected is systematically illegitimate. Change doesn't matter, when the deficit is at a systematic level. I'm using the strong point to beat up the weak point, and so I'm mentioning both.

And, put together for your convenience:

A weak point is that we 'can and do' 'regularly remove governments', so the UK doesn't have a democratic deficit. For example, Kier Starmer's Labour was elected in the 2024 General Election, removing the Conservatives from government. This happened because of deep dissatisfaction with the Conservative's record in government, such as from party-gate, or Truss' 50 days in government. This is further demonstrated by Starmer getting less of the vote than Corbyn did in the 2019 General Election: showing that it was not Labour's popularity that won the General Election, but the Conservative's unpopularity. A similar thing occurred in the 2010 and 1997 General Elections. A stronger point is that UK democracy has a 'problem with legitimacy' with our elected governments. For example, in the 2024 General Election, Labour got ~30% of the vote, but ~60% of the seats. Additionally, the Lib-Dems got 12% of the vote and 72 seats, yet Reform UK got 14% of the vote and 5 seats. This demonstrates that First Past The Post gives an unfair 'winner's bonus', which then causes the makeup of parliament to be disproportionate when compared to the popular vote. Overall, it's clear that UK elections do not elect legitimate governments: and so it does not matter if the electorate 'can' remove a government, because even if they manage: the one they elect in place almost always has power disproportionate to it's actual mandate because of the FPTP voting system: therefore, no matter if the government can change, it is systematically illegitimate. Therefore, the UK has a systematic democratic deficit to a large extent.

Shortened version:

A weak point is that we 'can and do' 'regularly remove governments'. For example, Kier Starmer's Labour was elected in the 2024 General Election, removing the Conservatives from government, similar things occurring in the 2010 and 1997 General Elections. This happened because of deep dissatisfaction with the Conservative's record in government. This is further demonstrated by Starmer getting less of the vote than Corbyn did in the 2019 General Election: showing that it was not Labour's popularity that won the General Election, but the Conservative's unpopularity — and so their consequent removal from government demonstrates a strong democracy. A stronger point is that UK democracy has a 'problem with legitimacy' with our elected governments. For example, in the 2024 General Election, Labour got ~30% of the vote, but ~60% of the seats. No government since the 1950's has been elected on more than 50% of the vote. This demonstrates that First Past The Post gives an unfair 'winner's bonus', which then causes the makeup of parliament to be disproportionate when compared to the popular vote. Overall, it's clear that — even if the electorate can change the government — FPTP often elects majority governments on a minority vote: which makes governments systematically illegitimate: proving that UK democracy has a democratic deficit to a large extent.
